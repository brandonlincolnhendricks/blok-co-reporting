Why Angelenos Are Moving to Austin

The LA to Austin pipeline is not a fad. It is driven by a stack of reasons that line up especially well for high-earning California households, and the flow has stayed strong into 2026.

The Financial Reset

For most Angelenos, the headline is money. Texas has no state income tax, while California's top rate reaches 13.3 percent. On top of that, the equity from selling a California home buys substantially more house in most of Austin. That combination, lower taxes plus housing arbitrage, is the number one reason California buyers tell us they made the move.

The Tech and Job Migration

Austin's technology economy has grown for years, and many of the employers, teams, and remote roles that anchor Los Angeles careers now have a real Austin footprint. For a lot of our clients, the company made the first move and the family followed, or a remote role freed them to choose where to live.

Space, Green, and a Slower Pace

After LA, the trees, lakes, and greenbelts surprise people. Austin trades coastline for the Hill Country, Lake Austin, Lake Travis, and the Barton Creek greenbelt, with a friendlier, less guarded pace of daily life. Families especially tell us the day-to-day feels calmer here.

The Tax and Housing Math

This is the part LA buyers want run carefully, so we model it for every relocation client. The two big levers are state income tax and the price of a comparable home.

On income tax, the swing is real. A household earning $300,000 to $500,000 or more can keep roughly $15,000 to well over $40,000 a year that California would have collected. Texas funds itself differently, mainly through property tax, so part of that saving comes back: in premium areas like the Westlake corridor, the combined effective property tax rate (city, Travis County, and school district) typically runs in the roughly 1.7 to 2.0 percent range of assessed value, with Travis County's adopted FY2026 county portion at about 0.3758 per $100. Even so, for most high earners the net still favors Texas.

Then there is the housing arbitrage. The equity from a Los Angeles sale generally stretches further in Austin, which is how a family can upgrade space, schools, or both while lowering their overall tax burden. The exact result depends entirely on your income, your LA sale price, and your Austin target, which is why a headline tax rate is never the whole story.

Where LA Buyers Land in Austin

Los Angeles is not one place, and neither is Austin. Where our LA clients end up usually comes down to schools, commute, and how close to the action they want to be.

Westlake (West Lake Hills)

For families chasing top schools, Westlake is the default. Inside the 78746 ZIP, it is served by Eanes ISD, which Niche ranked the number one district in Texas and among the top seven nationally for 2026, with Westlake High among the top public high schools in the state. It is also a 10 to 20 minute drive to downtown. The 2026 median sale price runs around $2.4 million, with the city of West Lake Hills itself near $2.8 million, so it is the premium choice for buyers who want schools and proximity together.

Central and Close-In Neighborhoods

Buyers who loved the walkability and energy of LA's Westside often gravitate to Tarrytown and other central neighborhoods near Lake Austin and downtown. You stay close to restaurants, parks, and the water without committing to a long commute.

Lake Travis and the Western Suburbs

For buyers who want newer construction, larger lots, and more home per dollar, the Lake Travis communities such as Lakeway deliver, in exchange for a longer 30 to 40 minute commute downtown. Lake Travis is a fluctuating reservoir, while Lake Austin holds a constant level, which matters if waterfront is on your list.

East and Creative Districts

Entertainment and creative professionals, the crowd that gave LA its energy, often land in East Austin and the central districts closest to the live-music scene. It is the part of town that feels most like a creative city in its own right.

Lifestyle: From LA to Austin

The lifestyle change is the part LA clients underestimate, in both directions. Some things get dramatically better, and a few things you will genuinely miss.

Entertainment to Live Music

Los Angeles is built around the entertainment industry. Austin is built around live music, calling itself the Live Music Capital of the World, with a year-round calendar of shows and festivals. The trade is studios and red carpets for a deep, hands-on music and creative culture that is woven into everyday life rather than walled off in an industry.

Traffic and Daily Logistics

For most movers, Austin traffic is a relief after LA. The metro is smaller, commutes are shorter, and the all-day gridlock of the 405 or 101 simply is not the daily reality here. Austin has its own bottlenecks (I-35 through the core, MoPac, and Loop 360 near the Pennybacker bridge), but the scale of congestion is a step down from LA.

Schools and Family Life

Austin gives families strong public-school options that, in areas like Eanes ISD, rank at the very top of the state and near the top nationally. For LA parents weighing private tuition against a public alternative, a top-ranked Texas district inside a no-income-tax state can reframe the entire household budget.

Honest Tradeoffs

We would rather you hear the caveats from us than discover them after you have unpacked the truck.

The Climate and the Coast

This is the big one. Los Angeles has a dry, mild climate and the Pacific in your backyard. Austin summers are hot and humid, and the nearest coastline is a flight or a long drive away. The lakes and greenbelt are wonderful, but they are not the ocean, and that is the single most common thing our LA clients miss.

Property Taxes Are Higher

No state income tax does not mean no taxes. Texas leans on property tax, and in premium areas the combined effective rate runs roughly 1.7 to 2.0 percent of assessed value. On a higher-priced home that is a real annual number, so it belongs in your budget from day one. Texas does offer a homestead exemption on a primary residence and the right to protest your assessed value each year, and Ross's appraisal background is genuinely useful when a protest comes down to defensible comparables.

Growth and Pace of Change

Austin is growing fast, and that brings traffic pressure, construction, and a market that moves. It is part of the appeal and part of the adjustment. Choosing the right neighborhood near your daily routes matters more here than newcomers expect.

Frequently Asked Questions: Moving to Austin from Los Angeles

How much can I save moving from Los Angeles to Austin?

The single largest saving is the elimination of state income tax. California's top rate reaches 13.3 percent, while Texas has none. A household earning $300,000 to $500,000 or more can keep roughly $15,000 to well over $40,000 a year that California would have taken. Texas property taxes claw back part of that, since combined effective rates in premium areas like the Westlake corridor run roughly 1.7 to 2.0 percent of value. Where do LA transplants live in Austin?

Most Los Angeles transplants we work with cluster in a handful of areas. Westlake and West Lake Hills, inside the 78746 ZIP, draw families who want the top-ranked Eanes ISD and a 10 to 20 minute downtown commute, with a 2026 median sale price around $2.4 million. Tarrytown and central neighborhoods appeal to buyers who want a walkable, close-in feel reminiscent of the Westside. Lake Travis communities such as Lakeway suit buyers who want newer construction and more space per dollar. Creatives and entertainment professionals often gravitate toward East Austin and central districts near the music scene. The right fit depends on schools, commute, and budget.

Is Austin traffic better than LA?

For most people moving from Los Angeles, yes, Austin traffic feels noticeably easier. Austin is a smaller metro with shorter average commutes, and you are far less likely to face the hours-long gridlock that defines LA freeways like the 405 or 101. That said, Austin is growing fast and has its own pressure points: I-35 through the core, MoPac (Loop 1), and Loop 360 near the Pennybacker bridge can back up at rush hour. The honest comparison is that Austin trades LA's scale of congestion for a few concentrated bottlenecks, and choosing a home near your daily routes matters more here than it might seem.

What do Angelenos miss or love about Austin?

Angelenos tend to love Austin's live-music culture, the green space and lakes, the lower cost of living, no state income tax, and a friendlier, less guarded pace of daily life. The trade for entertainment-industry energy is a deep, year-round music and festival scene rather than studios and red carpets. What people miss most is the ocean and the dry, mild Southern California climate: Austin summers are hot and humid, and the beach is a flight away, not a drive. Most relocators tell us the lifestyle gains and the financial math outweigh what they give up, but it is an honest tradeoff worth weighing.
