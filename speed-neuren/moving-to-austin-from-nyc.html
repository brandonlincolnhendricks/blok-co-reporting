Why New Yorkers Are Moving to Austin

The New York to Austin flow is driven by two engines: finance and tech. Wall Street firms and the funds and family offices around them have built a real presence in Texas, and the tech employers that anchor Austin keep pulling engineers, product leaders, and founders south. For a lot of our clients the trigger is a job or a fund relocating, but the deeper reason is almost always the same calculation.

The Income Math Adds Up Differently

New York is one of the highest-tax environments in the country once you stack the state income tax and, for city residents, the New York City income tax on top of it. Texas has no state income tax at all. For a high earner, that gap alone can fund a meaningfully larger life. When you pair it with what your housing budget buys in Austin versus Manhattan or Brooklyn, the move starts to look less like a lifestyle choice and more like a financial one.

Space, Sun, and a Different Pace

Beyond the spreadsheet, New Yorkers come for room to breathe. A downtown condo with a real layout, a central home with a yard, or a Hill Country property with views, all for what a smaller New York apartment costs. Add Lady Bird Lake, the greenbelt, live music, and a warm-weather outdoor life, and the appeal is easy to understand. Many of our clients describe it as keeping the urban energy they like about New York while trading the friction for space.

The Tax Math: New York and NYC Taxes vs None in Texas

This is where almost every conversation begins, so let us be precise. New York State's top income tax rate sits around 10.9 percent for the highest brackets. New York City residents then pay a separate city income tax on top of the state rate, which residents of Westchester, Long Island, or New Jersey suburbs do not. Texas has neither a state nor a local income tax. For someone earning a finance or tech compensation package, that combined New York burden is often the single largest line item that simply disappears in Texas.

The tradeoff, and we always say this plainly, is property tax. Texas funds itself partly through higher property taxes since there is no income tax to lean on. In the Austin area, combined effective property tax rates often run in the rough range of 1.7 to 2.0 percent of assessed value depending on jurisdiction, with Travis County's adopted FY2026 county rate at about 0.3758 per $100 of taxable value. That belongs in your budget from day one. Even so, for high earners the net almost always favors Texas, because income tax savings on a large salary dwarf the property tax on a single home.

The New York relocation math

Eliminating New York State income tax (top rate around 10.9 percent) and the additional New York City income tax can save a high-earning household anywhere from roughly $20,000 to well over $50,000 a year, depending on income and bracket. Texas property taxes claw back part of that, but for finance and tech earners the net still favors Texas, and once you add the housing arbitrage, where equity from a Manhattan or Brooklyn sale buys far more home in Austin, the first-year financial swing can be substantial. We model the full picture for every relocation client, not just the headline rate, so confirm the numbers for your specific situation.

Where NYC Buyers Land in Austin

There is no single answer to where New Yorkers buy, because the people leaving New York want different things. Broadly, our clients sort into three landing spots.

Downtown and the Condo Towers

This is the most natural fit for someone coming straight from a Manhattan or Brooklyn apartment. The downtown core, Rainey Street, and the South Congress towers offer high-rise living, building amenities, walkability, and a lock-and-leave lifestyle that feels closest to the New York rhythm. You can walk to restaurants, bars, and the lake trail, and you trade a yard for a view. For ex-New Yorkers who want urban density without the New York price per square foot, downtown condos are usually the first place we show.

Central Austin Neighborhoods

Buyers who want character and a little more room without leaving the urban orbit gravitate to central neighborhoods: Tarrytown and Clarksville near Lake Austin, Hyde Park north of the university, and the planned, walkable Mueller community on the east side. These areas give you a real home, walkable pockets, and a short drive downtown, which suits New Yorkers who liked their brownstone block more than their commute.

Westlake for Families and Schools

When the move is about kids and schools, families head to Westlake (West Lake Hills) in Eanes ISD, which Niche ranked the number one school district in Texas and among the top seven nationally for 2026, with Westlake High among the top public high schools in the state. Westlake trades urban walkability for top schools, wooded Hill Country lots, and a 10 to 20 minute downtown commute. It is the priciest part of the metro, and for school-focused New York families it is often worth it.

Adjusting from Transit to a Car City

The hardest practical adjustment for most New Yorkers is not the heat or the tax forms. It is the car. New York runs on the subway. Austin does not have a comparable rail network, so daily life is organized around driving and around distances rather than train lines. This is the single change our New York clients underestimate most.

There is one real exception. If you live in a downtown, Rainey Street, or South Congress condo and your work and social life are nearby, you can lean on walking and ride-share and delay buying a car. Some of our clients do exactly that for the first stretch. But most eventually buy one anyway, because groceries, weekend Hill Country trips, the airport, and visiting friends across town are all simpler with a car. If walkability matters to you, choose your neighborhood with that in mind, and remember to factor parking into any condo decision.

What changes when you trade transit for a car

  • You budget for a car: Purchase or lease, insurance, gas, and parking become real line items most New Yorkers did not carry.
  • Location is measured in drive time: Distances replace subway stops, so a home's commute and errands math matters more than walk score.
  • Downtown is the car-light exception: The condo towers are the closest thing to a no-car lifestyle, but parking still factors into the building you pick.
  • Weekends open up: A car turns the Hill Country, the lakes, and wine country into easy day trips.

The Honest Tradeoffs

We would rather you hear the caveats from us than discover them after the move. Austin is a genuinely great fit for a lot of New Yorkers, but it is not New York, and some of the differences cut both ways.

No Subway, Real Driving

We will say it again because it matters: the absence of comprehensive transit reshapes daily life. If a car-free existence is non-negotiable for you, only the downtown condo lifestyle comes close, and even that has limits.

Summer Heat Is Long

Austin summers are hot and they run long. Shade, a pool, and serious air conditioning go from nice-to-have to important. Many newcomers adjust their routines to mornings and evenings outdoors in the peak months.

Property Taxes Replace Income Taxes

The no-income-tax win comes with a higher property tax bill, so the savings show up in your paycheck and the cost shows up on your home. For most high earners the trade is favorable, but it changes how you budget for a house.

Growth and Change

Austin has grown fast, and that brings traffic on key corridors and a city that is still evolving. Most ex-New Yorkers find the pace refreshing rather than jarring, but it is a different texture than a mature, fully built city.

Frequently Asked Questions About Moving to Austin from New York

How much can I save moving from New York to Austin?

The biggest saving is income tax. New York's top state rate reaches about 10.9 percent, and New York City residents pay an additional city income tax on top of that, while Texas has no state or local income tax at all. A high-earning household in finance or tech can keep tens of thousands of dollars a year that previously went to Albany and City Hall, often $20,000 to well over $50,000 depending on income. Housing adds to it: the equity from a Manhattan or Brooklyn sale typically buys far more space in Austin. Texas property taxes offset part of the gain, so confirm the full picture for your specific situation.

Where do New Yorkers live in Austin?

Ex-New Yorkers tend to cluster in three areas. Downtown and the Rainey Street and South Congress condo towers appeal most to people who want walkability, building amenities, and a lock-and-leave lifestyle that feels closest to a New York apartment. Central neighborhoods like Tarrytown, Clarksville, Hyde Park, and Mueller suit buyers who want character, walkable pockets, and a quick drive downtown. Families who prioritize top schools gravitate to Westlake (West Lake Hills) in Eanes ISD. The right fit depends on whether you want urban density, central charm, or schools and space.

Do I need a car in Austin?

For most of Austin, yes. Unlike New York, Austin does not have a comprehensive subway or commuter rail system, so daily life outside the urban core is built around driving. The exception is the downtown condo lifestyle: if you live in a Rainey Street, downtown, or South Congress tower and work or socialize nearby, you can get by with walking, ride-share, and the occasional rental for a while. Most former New Yorkers eventually buy a car, even downtown, because errands, weekend trips to the Hill Country, and the airport are all easier with one. Budget for parking when you choose a building.

What surprises New Yorkers about Austin?

A few things consistently surprise our New York clients. First, the lack of transit means you organize your life around a car and around driving distances rather than subway lines. Second, summer heat is real and runs long, so shade, pools, and good air conditioning matter. Third, property taxes are high because there is no income tax, which changes how you budget for a home. On the positive side, people are surprised by how much more space their money buys, the outdoor lifestyle around Lady Bird Lake and the Hill Country, and how quickly the city feels like home.
