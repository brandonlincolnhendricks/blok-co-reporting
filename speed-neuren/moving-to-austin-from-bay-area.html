Why Bay Area Professionals Are Moving to Austin

The Bay Area to Austin flow is not a fad. It is the product of a few forces that all point the same direction, and tech is at the center of all of them.

The Tech Employer Base Followed Them Here

Austin has spent the past decade building a serious technology employment base, with major hardware, software, semiconductor, and electric-vehicle operations across the metro. For many Bay Area professionals, relocating no longer means leaving their industry or even their employer. The work followed the talent to Texas, which removed the single biggest objection people used to have about leaving California.

Your Compensation Goes Further

Bay Area incomes are high, and so is the cost of keeping them. Moving to a state with no income tax and lower housing costs means the same paycheck stretches dramatically further. That gap is the engine behind the relocation, and we break the actual numbers down in the next section.

Lifestyle Without the Tradeoffs People Expect

Newcomers often expect to trade culture and outdoors for affordability. Instead they find live music, a deep food scene, lakes, greenbelts, and Hill Country trails minutes from downtown. The move tends to feel like an upgrade in space and quality of life rather than a compromise.

The Tax and Housing Math

This is the part that turns a maybe into a moving truck. California has one of the highest state income tax burdens in the nation, with a top marginal rate of 13.3 percent. Texas has no state income tax at all. For a high-earning Bay Area household, eliminating that line item alone can keep $15,000 to well over $40,000 a year in the family's pocket, depending on income.

Then comes the housing arbitrage. Equity built up in a Bay Area home frequently buys substantially more square footage, land, and home quality in Austin. A sale that funds a modest house on the Peninsula can fund a large, updated home on a wooded lot here, often with money left over.

The Bay Area relocation math

A household earning $300,000 to $500,000 or more can save $15,000 to well over $40,000 a year by escaping California's 13.3 percent top income tax. Texas property taxes claw back part of that, but for high earners the net still favors Texas, and once you add the housing arbitrage, where Bay Area sale equity buys more home in Austin, the first-year financial swing can land well into the tens of thousands. We model the full picture for every relocation client, not just the headline tax rate, and we always tell clients to confirm the exact numbers for their specific situation.

The honest counterweight is property tax. Texas funds local services through property taxes rather than income tax, so the rate is higher than what most Californians are used to. In premium areas like Westlake, the combined effective rate (city, Travis County, and school district) typically runs roughly 1.7 to 2.0 percent of assessed value, with Travis County's adopted FY2026 county portion at about 0.3758 per $100. That is a real annual number on a higher-priced home, so it belongs in your budget from day one. Two things soften it: the Texas homestead exemption on a primary residence, and the right to protest your assessed value every year. Ross's appraisal background is genuinely useful when a protest comes down to defensible comparable values.

Where Bay Area Buyers Land

Bay Area relocators are not all the same buyer, so they do not all land in the same place. These are the areas we steer clients toward most often, depending on what matters to them.

Westlake (West Lake Hills)

The default choice for families who lead with schools. Westlake is served by Eanes ISD, which Niche ranked the number one school district in Texas and among the top seven nationally for 2026, with all of its schools top-ranked and Westlake High among the top public high schools in the state. It is also a 10 to 20 minute drive to downtown. The 78746 ZIP is Austin's highest-priced market, with a 2026 median around $2.4 million, so it draws equity-rich Bay Area buyers who recognize that profile.

Lakeway and Lake Travis

For buyers who want lake life, newer construction, and more home per dollar. Lakeway sits farther west on Lake Travis and trades a longer commute for waterfront, resort amenities, and larger lots. Worth noting for Bay Area boaters: Lake Travis fluctuates with drought and rainfall, while Lake Austin is constant-level, a distinction we explain to every waterfront-minded client.

Leander and the Northwest Corridor

The value play for tech families. Leander and the broader northwest corridor offer newer construction and proximity to north Austin technology campuses at a meaningfully lower price point than the central luxury markets. It is a frequent landing spot for Bay Area families who want space and a new build without the Westlake premium.

Downtown and Central Austin

For professionals and couples who want walkability, condos or modern homes, and an urban lifestyle close to offices, dining, and music. Bay Area transplants used to city living often start here, then decide whether to stay central or move to the suburbs once schools enter the picture.

Quick fit guide for Bay Area buyers

  • Lead with schools and short commute: Westlake (Eanes ISD).
  • Want lake, newer homes, more space: Lakeway / Lake Travis.
  • Want value and a new build near tech: Leander and the northwest corridor.
  • Want walkable, urban living: Downtown and central Austin.

Lifestyle Differences to Expect

Beyond the spreadsheet, daily life changes in ways that are worth picturing before you commit.

Space and Pace

Most Bay Area transplants step up in square footage and lot size while stepping into a more relaxed pace. Yards, pools, and home offices that felt out of reach on the Peninsula become normal here.

Weather and the Outdoors

Austin trades the Bay Area's mild, foggy summers for hot ones and genuinely warm springs and falls. The flip side is a long outdoor season around the lakes, greenbelts, and trails, and far fewer gray days.

Community and Culture

People often comment on how friendly and easy the city feels compared with the Bay Area, and how central live music, food, and outdoor life are to the local rhythm. For families, school communities, especially in districts like Eanes, become a real center of social life.

Frequently Asked Questions About Moving to Austin from the Bay Area

How much can I save moving from the Bay Area to Austin?

The biggest single saving is state income tax. California's top rate reaches 13.3 percent, while Texas has none, so a high-earning household can keep roughly $15,000 to well over $40,000 a year that California would have taxed. On top of that comes housing arbitrage: equity from a Bay Area sale typically buys substantially more home in Austin. Texas property taxes claw back part of the income-tax saving, so the net depends on your income, home value, and spending. For many Bay Area transplants the first-year financial swing still lands well into the tens of thousands. We model the full picture, and you should confirm the exact numbers for your specific situation.

Where do Bay Area tech workers live in Austin?

Bay Area tech families cluster in a handful of areas that balance schools, commute, and value. Westlake (West Lake Hills) is the top choice for top-ranked Eanes ISD schools and a 10 to 20 minute drive to downtown. Lakeway and the Lake Travis area offer waterfront, newer construction, and more home per dollar. Leander and the northwest corridor draw families who want new construction and proximity to north Austin tech campuses at a lower price point. Downtown and the central neighborhoods suit professionals who want a walkable, urban lifestyle. The right fit depends on whether schools, space, commute, or city living matters most to you.

Is Austin cheaper than the Bay Area?

For most relocating households, yes, in the ways that matter most. Austin has no state income tax versus California's top rate of 13.3 percent, and housing generally costs less per square foot, so Bay Area sale equity stretches further here. The main offset is property tax: Texas funds local services through higher property taxes, and in premium areas like Westlake the combined effective rate runs roughly 1.7 to 2.0 percent of assessed value. Day-to-day costs like dining and services are also typically lower than San Francisco or the Peninsula. The honest answer is that the overall cost of living usually drops, but the savings depend on your income and the home you buy.

What surprises Bay Area transplants about Austin?

A few things consistently catch newcomers off guard. The summer heat is real, with long stretches above 100 degrees, so shade, pools, and good HVAC matter. Property taxes are higher than many expect, which surprises people focused only on the income-tax win. Traffic on key corridors like Loop 360, MoPac, and I-35 can be heavier than the Hill Country image suggests. On the positive side, transplants are often surprised by how much more home their money buys, how friendly the city feels, and how strong the live-music, outdoor, and food scenes are. We walk relocation clients through these realities before they commit so there are no surprises after closing.
