Spicewood is a Hill Country community west of Lake Travis, spread across Travis and Burnet counties and centered on the 78669 ZIP code. It is anchored by acreage estates, rolling vineyard and ranch land, and lake access. The gated Spanish Oaks golf community sits just east in neighboring Bee Cave. Privacy and luxury on land are the through-line.

The Spicewood Hill Country Lifestyle

Spicewood is not the suburbs and it is not pretending to be. This is rural Hill Country: rolling terrain, oak and cedar cover, long views, and the kind of dark night sky you cannot get closer to the city. Buyers come here to spread out, and the lifestyle follows from the land.

Space, Privacy, and Quiet

Lots in Spicewood are large, often a full acre to many acres, so homes sit back from the road and neighbors are not on top of one another. The trade for that privacy is distance: shopping, dining, and services cluster toward Bee Cave and Lakeway, roughly 20 to 35 minutes east depending on where you are. For many of our clients, that distance is the whole point.

Lake and Outdoor Access

Spicewood touches Lake Travis and Lake Travis tributaries, with marinas, boat access, and lake-frontage and lake-access homes for buyers who want the water as part of daily life. Beyond the lake, the area is ringed by parks, trails, and the open Hill Country, and Krause Springs is a beloved local swimming and camping spot. Keep in mind Lake Travis is a fluctuating reservoir, not a constant-level lake, so waterfront usability changes with lake levels and is worth understanding before you buy on the water.

Wine Country Within Reach of Austin

Spicewood sits in the heart of the Texas Hill Country wine corridor. Wineries, vineyards, and tasting rooms are part of the local fabric, and weekends here often revolve around them. That wine-country character, combined with proximity to Austin, is a large part of why the area attracts buyers who want a more rural, estate-driven life without leaving the metro behind.

Spanish Oaks Gated Golf Luxury

If Spicewood is the land, Spanish Oaks, in neighboring Bee Cave just to the east, is the crown jewel of gated luxury in this stretch of the Hill Country. It is one of the most exclusive addresses in the greater Austin Hill Country, and it deserves its own section.

The Guard-Gated Community

Spanish Oaks is a guard-gated, members community in Bee Cave built around a private Bobby Weed-designed golf course on dramatic Hill Country terrain. Behind the gate are custom estate homes on large, view-oriented lots, a strong emphasis on security and privacy, and a private club lifestyle. It is the answer for buyers who want acreage-scale luxury and the assurance of a controlled, gated environment in one place.

What Drives Value Inside Spanish Oaks

Pricing in Spanish Oaks runs well into the multimillion-dollar range, and the spread between homes can be wide. Homesite location, golf-course frontage, the quality and length of the view, lot size, and the level of custom finish all move value significantly. This is exactly where Ross's appraisal background matters: a Spanish Oaks home cannot be priced off a generic per-square-foot average, because the lot and the view often matter as much as the house. We price off the right comparables, not the easy ones.

Acreage Estates, Wineries, and Vineyards

Outside the gates, Spicewood is defined by land. Acreage estates, working and hobby vineyards, ranchettes, and gentleman's ranches make up much of the inventory, and buying land well here is a different skill than buying a suburban home.

Acreage Estates and Ranchettes

Many Spicewood properties pair a custom home with several acres or more, offering room for horses, a vineyard, a guest house, a shop, or simply privacy. Some tracts are improved with utilities and a homestead in place; others are raw or lightly improved land sold by the acre. Access (paved versus easement), water availability, and deed restrictions vary widely and are central to value, so each tract has to be evaluated on its own terms.

Vineyards, Wineries, and Agricultural Use

The area's vineyards and wineries are not just amenities; for some owners they are part of the property itself. Agricultural use can affect taxation through ag valuation, and a working vineyard or ranch operation carries its own considerations. If you are buying land with agricultural or vineyard potential, the use, water rights, and any ag valuation status are all worth confirming up front.

Spicewood Home and Acreage Prices in 2026

Spicewood covers an unusually wide price range because it spans modest homes, acreage estates, gated luxury, and raw land. Entry-level homes and smaller lots can start in the high $500,000s to around $800,000. The broad heart of the market for updated acreage homes generally runs from about $1 million to $3 million, while estates, larger acreage tracts, lake-access homes, and Spanish Oaks residences regularly trade from $3 million into the $8 million-plus range.

Schools and the Realities of Rural Hill Country Property

Two things surprise newcomers to Spicewood more than anything else: the school district question and the rural infrastructure question. We would rather you understand both before you fall in love with a property.

Lake Travis ISD or Marble Falls ISD

Spicewood is served by Lake Travis ISD or Marble Falls ISD depending on exactly where a property sits, because the community straddles a district boundary across Travis and Burnet counties. This is not a detail to assume. The district determines which schools your children attend and can affect both taxes and resale demand, and two homes a short distance apart can fall in different districts. Do not assume a given home is in a particular district based on the Spicewood name alone. We confirm the exact district and campus assignment for every property we show.

Water and Septic

Rural property comes with infrastructure that suburban buyers rarely think about. Many Spicewood homes are on private wells or a water supply corporation rather than full city water, and most are on septic or aerobic wastewater systems rather than municipal sewer. Well yield, water quality, septic type and capacity, and the age of every system matter and should be inspected. None of this is a dealbreaker; it is simply due diligence that a suburban purchase would not require.

MUD and PID Districts

Some Spicewood and Spanish Oaks neighborhoods sit inside a Municipal Utility District (MUD) or Public Improvement District (PID) that funds water, roads, or amenities through additional assessments. These districts can add to your effective tax or assessment burden, and the terms vary by community. Confirm whether a specific property is inside a MUD or PID and what the obligation is before you make an offer.

Rural due-diligence checklist for Spicewood

  • School district: Lake Travis ISD or Marble Falls ISD, confirmed per property.
  • Water: private well, water supply corporation, or district water, with yield and quality checked.
  • Wastewater: septic or aerobic system type, capacity, and condition inspected.
  • Tax districts: any MUD or PID assessment confirmed and budgeted.
  • Access and restrictions: paved road versus easement, plus any deed or HOA restrictions.

Property Taxes and the Texas Tradeoff

Texas has no state income tax, and the tradeoff is higher property taxes, which matters for Spicewood buyers in a specific way. Because the area spans two counties and two possible school districts, and because some neighborhoods carry MUD or PID assessments, the combined rate on any given property can vary more here than in a built-out city. The county, school district, any district levies, and ag valuation status all factor in.

For Spicewood buyers, the right move is to confirm the exact combined rate and any district assessments for the specific property rather than relying on an area average. Texas also allows a homestead exemption on a primary residence and the right to protest your assessed value each year, and Ross's appraisal background is genuinely useful when a protest comes down to defensible comparable values. We help clients with both.

Who Buys in Spicewood

Spicewood and Spanish Oaks attract a distinct buyer. These are people choosing land and privacy on purpose, and they generally fall into a few profiles.

Land and Privacy Seekers

Many buyers come to Spicewood specifically for acreage: room for horses, a vineyard, a shop, or simply distance from neighbors. Some are trading a smaller lot in Westlake, Lakeway, or Bee Cave for real land, accepting a longer drive in exchange for space.

Gated-Luxury and Golf Buyers

Spanish Oaks draws buyers who want estate-scale luxury with the security of a guard gate and a private golf lifestyle. For this profile, the gate and the club are as important as the home itself.

Relocating and Equity-Rich Buyers

Executives and equity-rich buyers relocating from California, the Pacific Northwest, and the coasts are drawn by no state income tax and by how far their equity goes when it buys Hill Country land. For high earners leaving a state like California, where the top income tax rate reaches 13.3 percent, the annual income-tax saving alone can run from $15,000 to well over $40,000, and land in Spicewood lets that capital buy space rather than a small urban lot.
