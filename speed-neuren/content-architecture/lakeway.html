Lakeway is a resort-style city on the southern shore of Lake Travis, roughly 30 to 40 minutes west of downtown Austin in the heart of the Hill Country. It is anchored by the highly ranked Lake Travis Independent School District and is the center of a constellation of master-planned and waterfront communities that draw both full-time families and second-home buyers.

The Lakeway and Lake Travis Lifestyle

Lakeway is built around the water. Lake Travis is one of the largest reservoirs in the Highland Lakes chain, and life here is organized around boating, paddleboarding, lakeside dining, marinas, and long Hill Country evenings. It is the part of greater Austin where a Tuesday after work can include a sunset cruise and a Saturday can disappear entirely onto a boat.

Resort Amenities Without the Resort Price

The area packs in amenities that usually belong to a vacation destination: championship golf, full-service marinas, community pools and lazy rivers, tennis and pickleball, miles of trails, and a wellness and spa culture anchored by the longtime Lakeway resort scene. Several communities operate their own clubs, so daily life can feel like a private retreat that happens to have great schools.

Hill Country Beauty

Beyond the lake, this is classic Texas Hill Country: limestone bluffs, cedar and oak, long views, and dark-enough skies that you can actually see stars. Many homes are positioned to capture water or canyon views, and the terrain gives the whole area a sense of openness that the denser parts of Austin cannot match.

A Genuine Community

For all the resort feel, Lakeway is a real city with full-time families, youth sports, farmers markets, and a strong sense of neighborhood. The lifestyle attracts a mix of retirees, remote professionals, growing families, and second-home owners, and that blend is part of what keeps the area lively year-round rather than seasonal.

Communities Within Lakeway and Lake Travis

Lakeway and the surrounding Lake Travis area are not one neighborhood. They are a set of distinct communities, each with its own price band, amenity package, and buyer. Knowing the differences is half of buying well here.

Rough Hollow

One of the marquee master-planned communities on Lake Travis, Rough Hollow is known for its lakeside Yacht Club and marina, a resort-style pool complex with a lazy river, an extensive trail system, and a strong calendar of community events. It draws families who want top-tier amenities and a newer home, with a range that spans approachable family houses up to substantial waterfront and water-view estates.

The Hills of Lakeway

A guard-gated, golf-centered community built around the Jack Nicklaus-designed courses at The Hills Country Club. It offers established luxury, mature landscaping, and a quieter, club-oriented lifestyle. The Hills is a favorite of buyers who prioritize golf, privacy, and a settled prestige address within Lakeway.

Flintrock Falls

An exclusive, gated golf community surrounding the Flintrock at Hurst Creek course, also tied to The Hills Country Club. Flintrock Falls is among the most prestigious enclaves in the area, with large custom homes, dramatic Hill Country lots, and a strong sense of exclusivity. It suits buyers seeking a luxury estate with golf-course living and gated security.

Other Lake Travis Pockets

Beyond these three, the broader Lake Travis area includes waterfront enclaves, condo and lock-and-leave options near the marinas, and established Lakeway neighborhoods that offer the schools and lifestyle at more attainable prices. Each pocket carries its own profile, and we help buyers match the community to how they actually plan to live.

Waterfront Homes and the Fluctuating Lake

Waterfront is the crown jewel of the Lake Travis market and also the most nuanced thing to buy correctly. The single most important fact a buyer needs to understand is that Lake Travis is not a constant-level lake. Unlike Lake Austin, which is held at a steady level, Lake Travis is a reservoir that rises and falls with drought and rain, sometimes by tens of feet.

That fluctuation matters. In an extended drought, the shoreline can recede dramatically, leaving some docks dry and limiting boat access for a season or longer. When the rains return and the lake refills, access and sentiment recover. For long-term owners this is simply the rhythm of the lake, but it means shoreline type, dock design and permitting, and how far a home sits above full pool are genuinely load-bearing factors in value, not cosmetic details.

This is exactly where Ross's appraisal background changes outcomes. Pricing a Lake Travis waterfront home off a generic per-square-foot average ignores the variables that actually drive value here. We evaluate waterfront across both high-water and low-water scenarios, so buyers understand what they are really getting and sellers price to the strengths of their specific shoreline.

Before you buy Lake Travis waterfront

  • Check the shoreline: Gentle, usable shoreline holds value better than a steep drop that is hard to access at low water.
  • Confirm the dock: Verify dock permits, condition, and how usable it remains when the lake is down.
  • Look at lake history: Review how the property's access has held up through past droughts, not just a full-pool listing photo.
  • Mind the elevation: How far the home and dock sit above full pool affects both views and access during low water.

Homes, Prices, and Value vs Westlake

The headline reason buyers choose Lakeway over Westlake is value. You generally get more home, more land, and newer construction per dollar on Lake Travis than you do in the built-out hills of West Lake Hills. The tradeoff is the longer commute, which we cover honestly below.

Pricing in the Lake Travis area spans a wide range. Many family homes in Lakeway communities run from roughly $700,000 to $2 million depending on the community, lot, and finishes. True waterfront with usable frontage, a private dock, and deep-water access generally starts around $2 million and climbs well past $5 million to $10 million or more for estate properties in premier locations. Water-view and water-access homes that are not directly on the shoreline cost meaningfully less while still delivering the lake lifestyle.

Those ranges hide real variation, so treat them as a starting point and confirm specifics for your situation. Two homes in the same community can differ dramatically based on view, lot, age, and proximity to amenities, and waterfront pricing adds shoreline and lake-level factors on top. We price off the right comparables, not the easy ones.

What your budget buys around Lake Travis (2026)

  • $700K to $1.2M: Family homes in established Lakeway neighborhoods and entry points into newer communities, inside Lake Travis ISD.
  • $1.2M to $2M: Larger or newer homes in Rough Hollow and similar communities, many with Hill Country or partial water views.
  • $2M to $5M: Waterfront, premium estates in The Hills and Flintrock Falls, golf-course and view lots.
  • $5M to $10M+: Trophy waterfront estates with deep-water frontage, private docks, and premier locations.

Schools: Lake Travis ISD

Lakeway and most of the surrounding communities are served by Lake Travis Independent School District, one of the highly ranked public districts in the Austin metro and across Texas. Lake Travis High School in particular carries a strong statewide reputation both academically and athletically, and the district is a major reason families choose the area rather than treating it only as a second-home market.

Rankings shift year to year across sources like Niche and the Texas Education Agency, so a family focused on a particular campus should confirm the current rating and, just as important, the exact attendance zone for the specific home they are considering. Within the Lake Travis area, which community feeds which school can influence both daily logistics and home value, and we map that out for clients before they buy so there are no surprises after closing.

Commute and the Honest Tradeoffs

We would rather you hear the caveats from us than discover them after closing. The lake lifestyle and the value are real, and so is the distance.

The Commute Is the Main Tradeoff

From most of Lakeway, downtown Austin is roughly 30 to 40 minutes outside of peak traffic, usually along Highway 71 or RM 620 and then MoPac or Bee Caves Road. At rush hour the 620 and 71 corridors can back up, and a worst-case commute can stretch closer to an hour. Austin-Bergstrom International Airport is generally 40 to 50 minutes away. If you commute downtown daily, test-drive the route at the time you would actually travel before choosing a community.

More Driving for Daily Errands

While the Hill Galleria and the growing retail around the 620 and 71 area cover most needs, some shopping, dining, and specialty services still mean a longer drive than they would in a close-in neighborhood. Many residents see this as a fair trade for the lake and the space, but it is worth weighing if convenience ranks high for your household.

The Lake Level and Hill Country Terrain

As covered above, Lake Travis fluctuates, which is a genuine consideration for the most water-dependent properties. The Hill Country terrain that delivers the views also brings steep lots, some homes on septic rather than sewer, and ordinary wildfire awareness in the wildland interface. None of this should scare a buyer off, but each item belongs in due diligence on a specific home, and we flag it as a matter of course.

Lifestyle in Lakeway

Living in Lakeway means mornings on the trails, afternoons on Lake Travis, and rounds at The Hills or Flintrock without leaving home. The area is residential and amenity-rich by design, with golf, marinas, pools, and clubs woven into the communities, plus the restaurants and lakeside spots that make the weekends here feel like a getaway. For families, the year often runs through Lake Travis ISD and its strong athletics and arts, a genuine center of community life. And for buyers coming from higher-cost states, the value of getting all of this for less than comparable close-in luxury is a large part of the appeal.
