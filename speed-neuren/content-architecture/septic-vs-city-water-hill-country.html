Septic, Well Water, and City Utilities in the Austin Hill Country

By The Speed & Neuren Group at Compass | Updated June 2026 | The #1 Compass team in Austin and the Austin Business Journal's #1 real estate team, 2022 to 2025

One of the first questions we ask on any Hill Country showing is a quiet one that most buyers never think to raise: how does this home get its water, and where does the wastewater go? Inside the city, you almost never think about it. Out here, on larger lots and in the hills west and southwest of Austin, the answer changes from property to property, and it can shape your maintenance budget, your renovation plans, and even how you use your land.

Why Water and Wastewater Vary in the Hill Country

Closer to downtown Austin and inside incorporated cities, homes are typically connected to municipal or district water and sewer. As you move into the Hill Country and onto larger acreage, the math of running pipe changes. Extending public water and sewer mains across hilly, rocky terrain to a handful of large lots is expensive, so many of these homes were built on private wells, septic systems, or both.

The result is a patchwork. Two homes on the same road can have completely different setups, and a neighborhood can be on city water but private septic, or on a water district with no centralized sewer at all. Because there is no single rule, the only reliable approach is to verify the configuration on the specific property you are considering, not the area in general.

The Common Combinations

In practice you will see a few patterns: full city or district water and sewer; city or district water paired with private septic; and private well water paired with private septic on the most rural, large-lot properties. Each combination carries its own ongoing costs and its own due diligence.

Septic Systems: What to Inspect and Maintain

A septic system treats and disposes of wastewater on the property itself, and it is one of the most important items to evaluate on a Hill Country home. There are two broad types you will encounter here.

Conventional vs Aerobic Systems

A conventional system uses a tank and a gravity drain field to disperse effluent into the soil. An aerobic treatment unit injects air to break down waste more aggressively and then sprays treated effluent across a designated area. Aerobic systems are common where soils are shallow over rock, which describes a lot of the Hill Country, and they require a maintenance contract with a licensed provider plus periodic reporting. That ongoing contract is a real, recurring cost, so factor it in.

What a Septic Inspection Should Cover

Before you buy, have a licensed inspector evaluate the tank, lines, and drain field or spray area, and have the tank pumped so its condition can be seen. Confirm the system's design capacity matches the number of bedrooms, locate the field so you know how much land it occupies, and gather pumping and service records. For aerobic systems, verify the maintenance contract is current and can transfer to you.

Septic red flags to price in

  • No permit or records: An unpermitted or undocumented system can mean costly compliance work.
  • Undersized for the home: A system sized for fewer bedrooms than the house has limits future use.
  • Lapsed aerobic contract: A neglected aerobic unit can need repair or replacement.
  • Soggy or odorous drain field: A sign of a failing field, which is one of the more expensive fixes.

Well Water: Quality, Supply, and Testing

Where there is no public water connection, a private well draws from the aquifer below the property. Well water can be excellent, but it is your responsibility as the owner, not a utility's, so testing and treatment matter.

Test Before You Buy

Have the water tested for bacteria and nitrates at a minimum, and in the Hill Country it is worth checking hardness and total dissolved solids as well, since limestone geology often produces hard water. Review the well log for the depth and date drilled, and assess the pump's age and condition. Many owners here run a softener or a filtration system, which is normal and not a deal-breaker, but you want to know what is in place and what it costs to maintain.

Think About Supply, Not Just Quality

Quality is half the picture; supply is the other half. Well yield can vary with drought and aquifer conditions, so ask about the well's recovery rate and whether the household has ever experienced low flow. Some properties have storage tanks to buffer demand. Confirm these details for the specific well rather than assuming, because aquifer behavior differs across the region.

City and District Utilities

Plenty of Hill Country and near-in homes are on municipal or special utility district water, and some are on centralized sewer. This is generally the lowest-maintenance setup for an owner, since a provider handles treatment, testing, and the mains. If a property is served by a water control and improvement district (WCID) or a municipal utility district (MUD), it is worth understanding the rates and any district-specific assessments, which can differ from straight city service.

Even on city or district water, the home may still be on septic rather than connected sewer, which is one of the most common combinations we see. Do not let the presence of public water lead you to assume there is public sewer. Confirm both independently.

Due Diligence Checklist Before You Buy

When a Hill Country property is on a well, septic, or both, we treat the inspection period as the moment to remove surprises. Here is the short list we run with our buyers.

Hill Country utility due diligence

  • Confirm the setup: Identify exactly what supplies water and where wastewater goes, in writing.
  • Septic inspection and pump: Verify type, capacity, condition, and records; locate the field.
  • Aerobic contract: Check that any maintenance agreement is current and transferable.
  • Well water test: Test for bacteria, nitrates, hardness, and total dissolved solids.
  • Well log and pump: Review depth, age, recovery rate, and any storage.
  • District details: Understand WCID or MUD rates and assessments if applicable.
  • Future plans: Match any addition, pool, or guest house to the system's capacity and footprint.

None of this should scare you off a Hill Country home. Well and septic properties are some of the most private and beautiful in the Austin area, and once you understand the systems, they run quietly for years. The point is simply to verify, not assume, on every property. Frequently Asked Questions

Do Hill Country homes have city water or septic?

It depends entirely on the property. Homes closer to incorporated areas and denser pockets are often on municipal or district water and sewer, while many Hill Country and large-lot homes farther out are on septic systems and sometimes well water. Some properties mix the two, for example city or district water paired with a private septic system. Because the setup is not consistent even within a single neighborhood, it is never safe to assume. We confirm the exact water and wastewater configuration on every Hill Country property we show, so our clients know what they are buying before they write an offer.

What should I check on a septic system before buying?

Start with the permits and the system type, whether it is a conventional gravity system or an aerobic treatment unit, since aerobic systems require ongoing maintenance contracts. Have the system inspected and pumped, confirm the tank size suits the home, and locate the drain field or spray area. Ask for pumping and service records, and verify the design capacity matches the number of bedrooms. For aerobic systems, confirm the maintenance contract is current and transferable. A licensed septic inspector should evaluate the tank, lines, and field, and any deficiencies should be priced into your offer or repaired before closing.

Is well water safe and reliable?

Private well water can be perfectly safe and reliable, but unlike city water it is not treated or monitored by a utility, so the responsibility falls on the owner. Before buying, have the water tested for bacteria, nitrates, and common Hill Country issues like hardness and total dissolved solids, and review the well log for depth and the pump's age and condition. Supply matters too, since yield can vary with drought and aquifer conditions. Many Hill Country owners run softeners or filtration. With proper testing, the right treatment, and routine maintenance, well water serves these homes well, but verify it for the specific property.

How does septic affect renovations and additions?

Septic systems are sized for a specific number of bedrooms and a defined wastewater load, so adding bedrooms, a guest house, a pool, or a major addition can require upgrading the system or expanding the drain field. The drain field or aerobic spray area also occupies usable land, which can limit where you can build, add a structure, or place a pool. Steep Hill Country lots and shallow soils over rock can make expansion costly. Before planning a renovation, confirm the system's capacity and footprint and check county permitting, so the design fits what the property can support.
