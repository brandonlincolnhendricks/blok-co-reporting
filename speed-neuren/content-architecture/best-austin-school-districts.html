Why School Districts Matter So Much in Austin Real Estate

In the Austin metro, the assigned school district and even the specific attendance zone can move a home's value more than almost any other single factor. Two homes that look similar on paper can differ meaningfully in price simply because one feeds a top-ranked campus and the other does not. Families pay for the schools, and that demand is capitalized directly into the price.

There are a few reasons this effect is so strong here. Texas has no state income tax, which lets families direct more of their income toward housing and the districts they want. Austin's strongest districts are often compact and built out, so supply inside the best boundaries is limited while demand keeps growing. And because Texas school assignment is address-based, the only reliable way to secure a specific district is to own or rent inside its lines, which makes the boundary itself a real estate asset.

The one rule we tell every family

District boundaries and attendance zones do not always follow neighborhood names, and they can be redrawn. Before you fall in love with a home, confirm the exact campus assignment for that specific address with the district, not just the listing. We do this on every property we show, because a single street can be the line between two very different feeder patterns.

Eanes ISD (The Gold Standard)

If there is a consensus best district in the Austin area, it is Eanes Independent School District. For 2026, Niche ranked Eanes the number one school district in Texas and among the top seven in the nation. All nine Eanes schools earned top state and national rankings, and Westlake High School is consistently named among the top public high schools in Texas.

Eanes serves Westlake, West Lake Hills, Rollingwood, and surrounding pockets of the 78746 ZIP code just west of downtown. It is a relatively small, built-out district, which is exactly why homes inside its lines command such a clear premium. The 78746 ZIP is Austin's highest-priced market, with a 2026 median around $2.4 million and a West Lake Hills city median near $2.8 million. For families set on Eanes, that premium is the price of entry, and historically it has held value well.

What you are paying for in Eanes

Beyond the rankings, Eanes families point to small class sizes, deep arts and athletics programs, and a community that organizes much of its year around the schools. The honest caveat is cost: this is the most expensive district in the metro, and the most attainable entry, an older home on a smaller lot, generally still starts well over a million dollars.

Lake Travis ISD

Lake Travis ISD is the district families most often weigh against Eanes, and it is excellent in its own right. Covering Lakeway, Bee Cave, and the communities along Lake Travis to the southwest, it pairs highly ranked schools with a lake and resort lifestyle that Eanes cannot match.

The tradeoff is location and lake behavior. Lake Travis ISD sits farther from downtown, often a 30 to 40 minute commute, and it is built around Lake Travis, which is a fluctuating reservoir rather than a constant-level lake like Lake Austin. Pricing spans a wide range, frequently from the high $500,000s for attached homes into the multiple millions for waterfront estates, so there is real room to find value here while staying in a top district.

Leander ISD

Leander ISD is one of the largest and fastest-growing districts in the metro, covering parts of Cedar Park, Leander, and northwest Austin. It is well regarded academically and, for many families, it is where strong schools meet attainable prices. Newer construction, larger lots, and a broad range of price points make it a frequent landing spot for buyers who want quality schools without an Eanes or Lake Travis budget.

Because the district is large and still growing, attendance zones here are especially worth confirming. New campuses open and boundaries shift as neighborhoods are built out, so the campus a home feeds today is something to verify rather than assume. We watch these changes closely for clients targeting specific Leander ISD schools.

Dripping Springs and Round Rock ISD

Two more districts round out the list of places we regularly steer families, each appealing to a different kind of buyer.

Dripping Springs ISD

To the southwest, Dripping Springs ISD has earned a strong and rising reputation while offering Hill Country space and a small-town feel. Buyers who want larger lots, newer homes, and a growing, well-rated district often find Dripping Springs a compelling value, with prices that frequently start in the $500,000s and climb with acreage. The tradeoff is distance from central Austin and a more rural pace, which many families consider a feature rather than a drawback.

Round Rock ISD

North of the city, Round Rock ISD is a large, highly regarded district spanning parts of Round Rock, North Austin, and Cedar Park. It offers a wide range of neighborhoods and price points, strong schools, and quick access to the tech corridor and major employers. For families who work north of downtown, Round Rock ISD often delivers excellent schools and meaningfully more home per dollar than the districts closer to West Austin.

A note on Austin ISD magnets

Austin ISD is the metro's central district and is large and uneven, but it operates several highly regarded magnet and specialty programs that can rival the suburban leaders for the right student. Magnet admission is program-based rather than purely address-based, so families considering this route should research each program's process directly. It is worth knowing about, but it works differently from buying into a boundary.

How District Choice Affects Price

The same square footage, the same finishes, and the same lot can carry very different prices depending on which district and campus the address feeds.

Watch the resale side too

A strong district does not only help you get in, it helps you get out. Homes inside top Austin-area districts tend to sell faster and hold value better through market cycles because there is a steady pool of families who must buy inside those lines. We factor future resale into the district conversation, not just today's purchase.

Choosing a Home by District

When schools lead the decision, we walk families through a simple sequence. First, decide what you truly need from a district: the very top rankings, a specific program, a manageable commute, or the most home for the budget. Second, narrow to the one or two districts that fit, because trying to keep every option open usually means overpaying or missing the right home. Third, and most important, verify the exact attendance zone for any home you are serious about, since a campus assignment can change a home's value and its fit for your family.

From there it becomes a normal home search, just anchored to the right boundary. The families who do best here are the ones who treat the district and feeder pattern as part of the property itself, confirm it for the specific address, and then negotiate on the home with a clear view of what the schools are worth.
