Living in Leander and Cedar Park, TX: Northwest Suburbs and New Construction

By The Speed & Neuren Group at Compass | Updated June 2026 | The #1 Compass team in Austin and the Austin Business Journal's #1 real estate team, 2022 to 2025

When a family tells us they want a newer home, good schools, and more square footage than they can find closer in, Leander and Cedar Park are almost always part of the conversation. We are Ross Speed and Lindsay Neuren, Austin natives and the founders of The Speed & Neuren Group at Compass, and these two northwest suburbs are where a lot of Austin's growth, and a lot of its best value, lives right now.

This guide is written the way we talk to our own clients: what daily life is actually like, where the value is, what homes cost, how Leander ISD shapes demand, what the commute really looks like, and who is buying out here. If you are weighing the northwest suburbs in 2026, this is the honest picture.

Leander and Cedar Park sit side by side northwest of downtown Austin, along the US 183 and 183A corridor. Cedar Park is the more established city, closer in and built out, while Leander is newer and still expanding, anchoring the northern end of Capital MetroRail. Both are served by the well regarded Leander Independent School District.

Why Families Choose Leander and Cedar Park

The northwest suburbs win on a simple combination that is hard to find closer to downtown: newer homes, room to grow, strong schools, and a price point that still works for first-time and move-up buyers. This is where a growing family can get the floor plan and the yard they want without stretching past their budget.

Family Value and Room to Grow

Both cities are built around family life. Newer neighborhoods come with parks, pools, playscapes, and trails inside the community, and the lots tend to be larger than what the same money buys in central Austin. For households who want bedrooms, a real backyard, and a garage, the value out here is genuine, and it is the number one reason buyers keep choosing the northwest corridor.

Tech Corridor Proximity

Leander and Cedar Park sit within reach of the northwest tech corridor along US 183, including The Domain and Apple's north Austin campus. For workers commuting to those employment centers rather than all the way downtown, the drive is very manageable, which has made these suburbs a natural landing spot for tech and professional families.

No State Income Tax

Texas has no state income tax. For families relocating from higher-tax states, that alone can keep tens of thousands of dollars a year in the household budget, money that can go toward a larger or newer home. It is part of why the value equation in the northwest suburbs feels so strong to buyers coming from out of state.

Parks, Trails, and Lifestyle

The area is rich in green space. Cedar Park and Leander both invest in parks, splash pads, and trail systems, and the larger Brushy Creek Regional Trail and nearby lake recreation are easy weekend draws. Add the H-E-B Center for concerts, hockey, and events, plus a steady stream of shopping and dining, and you get a suburb that gives families plenty to do without driving into the city.

New Construction and Value

If there is one thing the northwest suburbs are known for, it is new construction. Leander in particular has a deep pipeline of master-planned communities and builder inventory, which is exactly why move-up and first-time buyers who want a brand-new home gravitate here.

New construction is where our approach earns its keep. Builder pricing is not the same as resale value, and incentives, upgrade packages, and lot premiums can make two similar homes very different deals. Ross began his career in appraisals, so we look hard at whether a builder's price and incentives actually pencil out against comparable resales nearby. We represent buyers in builder transactions at no cost to you, and having us at the table from the first model-home visit consistently protects clients on price, upgrades, and contract terms.

Homes and Prices

Leander and Cedar Park are among the better-value markets in the Austin metro, which is the whole point for many of our clients. As a general 2026 guide, a large share of single-family homes trade in roughly the $350,000 to $600,000 range, with newer, larger, or premium master-planned homes reaching higher. Treat those as starting points: the market moves, and we will confirm current numbers for your specific search.

Within that range, Cedar Park's established neighborhoods can carry a modest premium over comparable Leander homes because they are closer in and more built out, while Leander's newer construction often delivers more square footage per dollar. Lot size, builder, community amenities, school feeder, and condition all move the number, sometimes by a lot. Pricing a home off a generic per-square-foot average is how buyers overpay and sellers leave money behind, so we price off the right comparables, not the easy ones.

Cedar Park

The more established city, with mature neighborhoods, a dense retail and dining core around 1890 Ranch and the H-E-B Center, and a shorter drive toward Austin. A strong fit for buyers who want a settled neighborhood and convenience over brand-new construction.

Leander

Newer and still growing, with the most active new construction, master-planned communities, larger lots for the money, and the northern MetroRail station. A frequent landing spot for buyers who want a new home and more space per dollar.

Schools: Leander ISD

Leander Independent School District serves both Leander and Cedar Park, and it is a major reason families choose the area. The district is well regarded across the Austin metro, with a reputation for strong academics, competitive athletics, and respected fine arts programs, and it operates a number of highly rated campuses spanning its high schools, middle schools, and elementaries.

Because both cities are growing, Leander ISD continues to add campuses and adjust attendance boundaries over time. That means the specific schools a home feeds into can differ from one neighborhood to the next, and zones can shift as the district expands. We always confirm current school assignment for any home a family is considering, since for many of our buyers the feeder pattern is as important as the house itself.

Why Leander ISD drives demand

For families, schools are often the first filter and the last word. Leander ISD's strong reputation supports both demand and resale value across the northwest suburbs, which is part of why well-located homes in good feeder patterns hold their value. We map the exact campus assignment, not just the district, before any family writes an offer, so there are no surprises after closing.

Commute, MetroRail, and Amenities

The commute is the honest tradeoff of the northwest suburbs, and it is worth being clear-eyed about it. From Cedar Park, downtown Austin is typically a 30 to 40 minute drive outside peak traffic; from Leander it is usually 35 to 50 minutes, mostly via US 183, the 183A toll road, and MoPac (Loop 1). The tech corridor and The Domain are closer, often 20 to 35 minutes, which is why many buyers out here are not commuting all the way downtown in the first place.

Leander also anchors the northern terminus of Capital MetroRail. Commuters can park and ride the train into downtown, which is a real advantage on office days and a feature unique to this part of the metro. We always suggest test-driving your specific route at the times you would actually be on the road, because rush hour on 183 and MoPac is the part newcomers most often underestimate.

Everyday Amenities

Day-to-day life is well covered. Shopping and dining cluster at 1890 Ranch and along the 183 corridor, the H-E-B Center hosts concerts, hockey, and events, and grocery, healthcare, and retail are close at hand in both cities. Combined with the parks and trails, it adds up to a suburb where families rarely need to drive into Austin for the basics.
