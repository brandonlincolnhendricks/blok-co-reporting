This guide walks through the protest the way we coach our own clients through it: why the assessment matters, how the Travis Central Appraisal District (TCAD) process and deadline work, the evidence that actually moves the needle, the homestead exemption and other relief, and how an appraiser-trained team helps you build a case the Appraisal Review Board takes seriously. None of this is legal or tax advice, and you should confirm the specifics for your own property, but it is the practical playbook.

Why Protesting Your Assessment Matters

Your property tax bill is the product of two things: your appraised value, set by TCAD, and the tax rates set by the taxing entities (the county, your school district, your city, and any special districts). You cannot change the rates by protesting, but you can challenge the value, and the value is the lever you actually control.

A Higher Value Compounds Every Year

Texas appraisal districts reassess values annually, and in a market like Austin that has appreciated sharply, valuations have climbed fast. If your appraised value is set too high in one year, you do not just overpay that year. You can carry an inflated baseline forward, because future increases build on top of it. Correcting an overstatement now protects you in the years to come, not just this one.

The Stakes in a High-Value Market

In the Westlake area and across the premium pockets of Travis County, combined effective property tax rates often land in roughly the 1.7 to 2.0 percent range of assessed value. On a home valued near $2 million, every $100,000 of overstated value can mean close to $2,000 a year in tax you should not be paying. That is real money, and it is why we treat the protest as a routine part of owning here, not a fight to be avoided.

Protesting Is Common and Expected

A protest is not an adversarial act. TCAD expects a large share of owners to protest, and the process is built to handle it. You are simply asking the district to show its work and to correct the value if the evidence supports a lower number.

The Travis County Protest Process and Deadline

The mechanics are straightforward once you know the sequence. TCAD is the agency that appraises every property in the county, and it is where your protest lives from start to finish.

Step One: Read Your Notice of Appraised Value

TCAD typically mails Notices of Appraised Value in the spring, often in April. The notice shows your new appraised value, your prior value, and instructions for filing a protest. Read it carefully and note your specific deadline, because it is printed right on the notice.

Step Two: File Before the Deadline

The Texas protest deadline is generally May 15, or 30 days after TCAD mails your notice, whichever is later. Most Travis County owners are working toward a mid-May date. You can file online through the TCAD portal, by mail, or in person. Our advice: file as soon as your notice arrives, even if your evidence is not finished, because you can supplement the case later and missing the deadline forfeits your rights for the year.

Step Three: The Informal Review

TCAD usually offers an informal meeting with a staff appraiser first. Many protests settle here. You present your evidence, the appraiser presents theirs, and if you reach an agreed value you are done. A clean, organized packet of comparables makes this conversation go faster and better.

Step Four: The Appraisal Review Board

If the informal review does not resolve it, your protest goes to the Appraisal Review Board (ARB), an independent panel of citizens. You present your case, TCAD presents theirs, and the board sets the value. It is less formal than a courtroom, but it rewards preparation and organized evidence.

Key dates and steps at a glance

  • April (typical): TCAD mails your Notice of Appraised Value.
  • On or about May 15: Protest filing deadline, or 30 days after your notice, whichever is later.
  • Spring to summer: Informal review with a TCAD appraiser.
  • Summer: Appraisal Review Board hearing if not settled informally.
  • Always: Confirm the exact deadline printed on your own notice.

Building Your Evidence (Comparables and Condition)

A protest is won on evidence, not opinion. The board has seen every version of "my taxes are too high." What persuades them is a tight, factual case. There are three pillars.

Comparable Sales

The core of most residential protests is recent comparable sales: homes near yours, similar in size, age, and quality, that sold for less than your appraised value implies. Raw sale prices are not enough. You adjust them for differences in square footage, lot size, condition, finish level, and view, then show what your home should be worth on that basis. This is the same work an appraiser does, and selecting the right comparables rather than the convenient ones is where cases are won or lost.

Condition and Defects

TCAD generally values from the outside and from records, so it often does not know about problems inside your home. Document anything that lowers value: a dated kitchen or bath, foundation or roof issues, plumbing or HVAC at end of life, a steep or awkward lot, drainage or flood exposure. Photos plus written repair estimates from contractors carry real weight and are frequently the difference maker on a home the district has not seen up close.

Factual Errors in the Record

Pull your property record from TCAD and check it line by line. Districts sometimes carry the wrong square footage, an extra bathroom, the wrong lot size, or an improvement that no longer exists. Correcting a simple factual error can lower your value without any argument about the market at all.

The Homestead Exemption and Other Relief

Protesting your value is one lever. Exemptions are the other, and many owners leave money on the table by not claiming everything they qualify for.

The Homestead Exemption

If a property is your primary residence, the Texas homestead exemption reduces its taxable value and lowers your bill. Just as important, it caps how much your assessed value can rise each year, currently 10 percent annually, so over time a homestead can keep your taxable value below true market value even when prices surge. You generally apply once with TCAD and the exemption carries forward year to year. If you have bought a home and not yet filed, this is the first thing to fix.

Exemptions for 65-Plus and Disabled Owners

Homeowners who are 65 or older or who are disabled qualify for additional exemptions, and in many cases the school-tax portion of their bill is frozen or further reduced. These can produce meaningful savings and are worth confirming with TCAD if you or a family member qualifies.

How Exemptions and Protests Work Together

The homestead cap and a successful protest are complementary. A protest corrects the market value the cap is measured against, and the exemption then limits how fast your taxable value can climb. Owners who do both, file every exemption they qualify for and protest in years when the value is overstated, generally pay the least over time.

The no state income tax tradeoff

Texas has no state income tax, and the tradeoff is property taxes that run higher than in many other states. That structure is part of why high earners relocating from places like California, where the top income tax rate reaches 13.3 percent, often still come out well ahead in Texas. But it also means your appraised value matters more here than almost anywhere. Filing your exemptions and protesting an overstated value are simply how you manage the cost side of a tax structure that, on the whole, favors Texas homeowners.

Frequently Asked Questions About Protesting Travis County Property Taxes

How do I protest my property taxes in Travis County?

You protest with the Travis Central Appraisal District (TCAD), the agency that sets your appraised value. After you receive your Notice of Appraised Value in the spring, file a protest before the deadline, usually around May 15, either online through the TCAD portal, by mail, or in person. TCAD typically offers an informal review with an appraiser first, where many cases settle. If you do not reach agreement, your protest goes to the Appraisal Review Board (ARB), an independent panel that hears your evidence. You are protesting the value, not the tax rate, so a lower value means a lower tax bill.

When is the deadline to protest?

In Texas the protest deadline is generally May 15, or 30 days after TCAD mails your Notice of Appraised Value, whichever is later. Notices typically go out in April, so most Travis County homeowners are working toward a mid-May deadline. If the date falls on a weekend or holiday it usually rolls to the next business day. Missing the deadline almost always forfeits your protest rights for that year, so the safe move is to file as soon as your notice arrives, even before your evidence is complete, because you can add to your case later. Always confirm the exact date on your own notice.

What evidence do I need to lower my assessment?

The strongest evidence is recent comparable sales: homes near yours that sold for less per square foot than your appraised value implies, adjusted for size, condition, lot, and finish. Beyond comparables, document anything that lowers value, such as deferred maintenance, foundation or roof issues, an outdated interior, a difficult lot, or flood exposure, with photos and repair estimates. Also check TCAD's record for factual errors in square footage, bedroom count, or land size. A clean, organized packet of adjusted comparables plus documented condition issues is far more persuasive than simply arguing the number feels too high.

What is the homestead exemption?

The Texas homestead exemption reduces the taxable value of your primary residence, which lowers your tax bill. It also caps how much your assessed value can rise each year, currently 10 percent annually, so a homestead can hold your taxable value below market even when home prices jump. Additional exemptions exist for homeowners who are 65 or older or disabled, and they can freeze or further reduce the school portion of taxes. You generally apply once with TCAD and the exemption carries forward. Filing your homestead exemption is one of the simplest, highest-impact tax moves a Travis County owner can make.
