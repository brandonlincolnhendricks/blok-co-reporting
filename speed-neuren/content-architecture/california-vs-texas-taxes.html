The Headline: No State Income Tax in Texas

The starting point is simple. Texas has no state income tax. California has one of the highest in the country, with a top marginal rate that reaches 13.3 percent on the highest incomes. That single difference is the gravitational center of almost every relocation conversation we have, and for good reason.

California's income tax is progressive, so the average effective rate a household actually pays is lower than the top marginal rate. But for the high earners who make up most of our relocation clients, the marginal dollars are taxed near the top of the schedule, which is where the savings concentrate. When that obligation drops to zero in Texas, the money does not disappear into a different state tax. It stays in the household budget, and for many of our buyers it goes straight into a larger or more comfortable mortgage in Austin.

Why the Top Rate Matters More Than the Average

If your income is climbing into the mid six figures or beyond, you are paying California's higher brackets on your last and most valuable dollars. Eliminating that marginal bite is what makes the Texas move so compelling for executives, founders, and dual-income professional households. The lower your income, the smaller the effect, which is why we always frame the savings by income level rather than quoting a single number.

The Property Tax Tradeoff (Honest Math)

Here is where we slow people down, because this is the part the relocation excitement tends to skip. Texas pays for the lack of an income tax with higher property taxes, and in a high-value market like the Austin Hill Country that is a meaningful annual number you must plan for from day one.

In the Westlake area, the total rate combines city, Travis County, and school district levies. Travis County's adopted FY2026 county rate is about 0.3758 per $100 of taxable value, and once the city and Eanes ISD rates are added, the combined effective rate in the Westlake area typically lands in roughly the 1.7 to 2.0 percent range of assessed value. On a $2.5 million home, that is a real five-figure annual obligation.

Two things soften it. The Texas homestead exemption reduces the taxable value on a primary residence, and homeowners have the right to protest their assessed value every year. Ross's appraisal background is genuinely useful here, because a protest comes down to defensible comparable values, which is exactly the work he did before real estate.

Do not skip this line in your budget

Property tax in the Westlake area runs roughly 1.7 to 2.0 percent of assessed value, so a $2.5 million home can carry $40,000 to $50,000 a year in property tax before any homestead exemption or successful protest. Always confirm the exact rate and exemptions for a specific property, because the jurisdiction and assessed value drive the number.

Housing Cost and Equity Arbitrage

The income-tax savings get the headlines, but housing arbitrage is often the quiet workhorse of the relocation math, especially for equity-rich households selling an expensive California home. When the proceeds from a coastal California sale land in Austin, they frequently buy a comparable or better home with cash left over, or they shrink the mortgage that the income-tax savings then service more easily.

Austin is not cheap. The 78746 ZIP code that covers Westlake is the highest-priced market in the city, with a 2026 median near $2.4 million, and the city of West Lake Hills runs higher, near $2.8 million. So the arbitrage is not about finding bargains. It is about a dollar of California home equity stretching further in Austin while no longer carrying a state income-tax burden on the income that services it.

How the Two Levers Compound

The reason the first-year swing can be so large is that the levers stack. Eliminated income tax frees up cash flow. Housing arbitrage either lowers the mortgage or frees up equity. Higher property tax pulls back against both, but for high earners the two positive levers usually outrun the one negative one. That is how a household can see a first-year financial swing in the $50,000 to $80,000 range or more once everything is added together, even after accounting honestly for the Texas property-tax bill.

The California relocation math

A household earning $300,000 to $500,000 or more can save $15,000 to well over $40,000 a year by escaping California's state income tax. Texas property taxes claw back part of that, but for high earners the net still favors Texas, and once you add the housing arbitrage, where California sale equity buys more home in Austin, the first-year financial swing can exceed $50,000 to $80,000. When Texas Clearly Wins and When It Is Closer

We are not in the business of selling everyone the same story. The tax move is a clear win for some households and a much closer call for others, and you deserve to know which one you are before you list your California home.

When Texas Clearly Wins

High earners are the clearest case. A household in the mid six figures or above, particularly one selling an expensive California home with substantial equity, tends to come out well ahead. The eliminated income tax is large, the arbitrage is meaningful, and the higher property tax is a manageable fraction of the total benefit. Add the lifestyle and schools that draw families to areas like Westlake, and the decision often makes itself.

When It Is Closer Than People Expect

The math narrows for lower-income households buying expensive Austin homes. If you earn modestly but stretch into a high-priced property, the property-tax bill can eat a large share, or even all, of the income-tax savings. The same is true for buyers coming from lower-cost California markets where the housing arbitrage is small. In those cases the move can still make sense for lifestyle, schools, or career reasons, but you should make it with eyes open rather than assuming the tax story carries it.

Putting It Together for a Relocation Budget

A real relocation budget is not a single tax rate, it is a balance of three numbers. Start with your income-tax savings based on your actual earnings, not the top California rate. Subtract your projected Texas property tax on the specific home you are considering, after homestead exemption. Then layer in the housing picture: what your California sale nets, what your Austin home costs, and how that changes your mortgage. The honest first-year swing is what falls out of those three lines together.

Frequently Asked Questions About California vs Texas Taxes

How much do high earners save moving from California to Texas?

The biggest single line is the elimination of state income tax. California's top rate reaches 13.3 percent while Texas has none. A household earning $300,000 to $500,000 or more can save roughly $15,000 to well over $40,000 a year in state income tax alone, and the figure climbs for very high incomes. Texas property taxes claw back part of that, but for high earners the net still favors Texas. When you add housing arbitrage, where California sale equity buys more home in Austin, the first-year financial swing can exceed $50,000 to $80,000. We model the full picture for every relocation client rather than quoting the headline rate, so confirm your specific numbers.

Does Texas property tax cancel out the income tax savings?

Not for most high earners, though it depends on income and home price. Texas property taxes are higher than California's, and in the Westlake area of Austin the combined effective rate typically runs roughly 1.7 to 2.0 percent of assessed value. That is a real annual cost, but it is offset by zero state income tax. For a household earning several hundred thousand dollars or more, the income-tax savings usually exceed the extra property tax. For a lower-income household buying an expensive home, the math gets closer and can even tip the other way, which is exactly why you should run your own numbers before assuming a clear win.

Is the cost of living really lower in Austin than California?

For most relocating households, yes, though not in every category. Texas has no state income tax, and on a like-for-like basis housing in much of Austin costs less than comparable coastal California markets, even though Austin itself is not cheap. The luxury 78746 ZIP code is Austin's highest-priced market, with a 2026 median near $2.4 million, so the savings come from arbitrage rather than absolute bargains. Property taxes are higher in Texas, and insurance and energy costs vary. The net effect for high earners is usually favorable, but the size of the gap depends on your income, your housing choice, and your spending, so it is worth modeling your specific case.

What income level makes the Texas move clearly worth it?

On taxes alone, the move tends to look clearly favorable once household income climbs into the mid six figures, roughly $300,000 and up, where the eliminated California state income tax outweighs higher Texas property taxes by a comfortable margin. Above $500,000 to $1 million the annual swing becomes substantial. Below that range the decision is more about lifestyle, schools, and housing value than pure tax math, and the property-tax tradeoff can narrow the gap. Equity-rich households selling expensive California homes also benefit from housing arbitrage regardless of income. These are general bands, not advice, so confirm your specific situation with your own advisors.
