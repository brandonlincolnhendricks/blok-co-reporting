Where to Find the Best Hill Country Views Near Austin

By The Speed & Neuren Group at Compass | Updated June 2026 | The #1 Compass team in Austin and the Austin Business Journal's #1 real estate team, 2022 to 2025

A view is the one thing about a home you can never add later. We are Ross Speed and Lindsay Neuren, Austin natives and the founders of The Speed & Neuren Group at Compass, and over more than a billion dollars in Central Texas sales we have learned that nothing moves a buyer, or a price, like standing on a back deck and looking out over the Hill Country. This guide covers where the best views are, what they add, and how we make sure clients pay the right number for them.

The hills west of downtown Austin are where the elevation, the canyons, and the lakes line up to create real long-range views. Below we map the strongest view areas, from the ridge lines of Westlake to the water and hill panoramas of the Lake Travis communities, and explain how an appraiser-trained eye changes the way a view gets priced.

Why Hill Country Views Command a Premium

A view is a fixed, scarce asset. You can renovate a kitchen, add a wing, or refinish a pool, but you cannot manufacture a ridge-line outlook over miles of cedar and limestone canyon. In the hills around Austin, that scarcity is concentrated on a relatively small number of elevated lots, and demand for them far outstrips supply.

There is a lifestyle component too. A western-facing view turns an ordinary evening into a sunset over the Hill Country, and a canyon outlook delivers privacy that a flat interior lot cannot. Buyers relocating from California, the Pacific Northwest, and the coasts often arrive specifically looking for that long view, and Texas has no state income tax, so high earners can direct more of their income toward the kind of premium lot a view requires.

What Makes One View Worth More Than Another

Not all views are equal. The most valuable share three traits: elevation that lifts the home above its surroundings, an unobstructed outlook that is unlikely to be built out, and an orientation, usually west or toward a canyon or lake, that frames something worth looking at. A protected, year-round panorama is worth a great deal more than a partial view that disappears when the trees leaf out or when a neighbor builds a second story.

Best View Areas in Westlake

Westlake (West Lake Hills) sits in the 78746 ZIP code, the highest-priced market in Austin, with a 2026 median sale price near $2.4 million and a West Lake Hills city median closer to $2.8 million. Its Hill Country topography is exactly why so many of those homes carry view premiums. It is also served by Eanes ISD, ranked the number one district in Texas and number seven nationally by Niche for 2026, so a view home here pairs scarcity with the area's strongest demand driver.

Rob Roy Ridge Lines

Rob Roy is a guard-gated estate community on some of the highest ground in Westlake. The upper streets capture long canyon and Colorado River corridor views, and in places the downtown skyline. These are among the most significant view lots in the area, paired with the largest homes.

Davenport Ranch

The elevated streets of Davenport Ranch look out over canyons and, on the lots that fall toward the water, Lake Austin itself. Davenport blends gated luxury and golf with both hill and water views, which is a rare combination this close to downtown.

Lost Creek and Westlake Highlands

Higher lots in Lost Creek and Westlake Highlands open onto the greenbelt and canyon below. These pockets are a more attainable way into a Westlake view while staying inside Eanes ISD, and the outlook over protected greenbelt land tends to be durable.

Best View Areas Around Lake Travis and Spanish Oaks

Move farther west and the views shift from canyons to water. The communities around Lake Travis add the lake itself to the Hill Country backdrop, and they generally offer newer construction and larger lots than built-out Westlake.

Lakeway

Lakeway sits on Lake Travis, and its elevated streets deliver both water and hill views. Lake Travis is a fluctuating reservoir, unlike constant-level Lake Austin, so the waterline shifts with drought and rain, something we always factor in when valuing a Lake Travis view.

Rough Hollow

Rough Hollow, the master-planned community on the south shore of Lake Travis in the Lakeway area, was laid out around its outlooks. Many homes capture sweeping lake and Hill Country views, paired with marina and resort amenities.

Spanish Oaks

Spanish Oaks in Bee Cave is one of the premier view communities in the region. Built around a private golf course and the surrounding canyons, its lots combine golf-course frontage with wide Hill Country panoramas, and the gated, low-density layout helps protect those outlooks over time.

Where the best views cluster

  • Rob Roy (Westlake): Highest ridge lines, canyon and skyline outlooks, estate homes.
  • Davenport Ranch (Westlake): Canyon and Lake Austin water views with golf.
  • Lost Creek / Westlake Highlands: Greenbelt and canyon views, more attainable entry.
  • Lakeway and Rough Hollow: Lake Travis water and hill views, newer homes.
  • Spanish Oaks (Bee Cave): Golf-course frontage plus sweeping Hill Country panoramas.

How Much a View Adds to Value

A genuine Hill Country view is one of the strongest price premiums in this market. As a working range, a quality view commonly adds somewhere on the order of 10 to 25 percent over an otherwise comparable home with no view, and a rare protected skyline or water panorama can push beyond that. These are general ranges, not a promise for any one property, and you should confirm the right figure for your specific lot.

The reason the range is so wide is that the word "view" hides enormous variation. An unobstructed, west-facing canyon view that cannot be built out behaves very differently from a partial, seasonal glimpse through a stand of oaks. The table below shows how we think about the gradient.

How We Price View Properties (Appraiser Insight)

A view is the single feature most often mispriced in the Austin market, and it is where Ross's background changes outcomes. Ross began his career in appraisals, so the team values a view the way an appraiser does: by isolating the view as a line-item adjustment between truly comparable sales, rather than smearing it into a generic per-square-foot average.

That matters in both directions. Sellers with a real, protected view routinely leave money on the table when an agent prices off square footage alone and never quantifies the outlook. Buyers, meanwhile, overpay when a seller bakes in a premium for a view that is partial, seasonal, or at risk of being built out. We pull the right comparables, weight the orientation and durability of the view, and put a defensible number on it.

The view questions we always ask

Is the outlook protected, or could a neighbor build up and block it? Is it year-round or seasonal through the trees? What is the orientation, and does it catch the sunset? On a Lake Travis lot, how does the fluctuating waterline affect the view in a dry year? And on a steep view lot, what do construction, drainage, and maintenance add to the true cost of ownership? These answers move the number, and we work through every one before anyone makes an offer.

Finding the Right View Home

Because view quality changes lot by lot and even house by house, the only reliable way to evaluate one is on site. We walk specific listings with clients, stand in the rooms that face the outlook, check the orientation against the sun and the season, and assess whether the view is protected for the long term. A floor plan and a listing photo cannot tell you any of that.

If a long Hill Country view is what brought you to Austin, we will help you find the lot that delivers it and make sure the price reflects what the view is actually worth. View homes also tend to hold and grow value well, since the view itself is scarce and cannot be reproduced, which makes getting the purchase price right the foundation of a smart long-term hold.
