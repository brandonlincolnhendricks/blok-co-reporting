Short-Term Rental Rules in Austin and the Hill Country [2026]

By The Speed & Neuren Group at Compass | Updated June 2026 | The #1 Compass team in Austin and the Austin Business Journal's #1 real estate team, 2022 to 2025

Short-term rentals are one of the most common questions we get from buyers eyeing the Austin metro, and one of the most misunderstood. We are Ross Speed and Lindsay Neuren, Austin natives and the founders of The Speed & Neuren Group at Compass, and over more than a billion dollars in Central Texas closings we have watched plenty of investors learn the hard way that an Airbnb or VRBO plan only works if the property is actually allowed to operate as one.

Here is the honest headline: there is no single rule for the whole area. Short-term rental rules vary by jurisdiction, and a home that can be licensed in one city may be flatly prohibited a mile away. This guide walks through how the framework is structured, where the lines fall across the cities and unincorporated areas we work in, and what you should verify before you write an offer. One caveat we put up front and will repeat: these rules change, and they have been actively litigated and revised in recent years, so always confirm the current local code for any specific property.

Why STR Rules Differ Across the Metro

The reason a clean, single answer does not exist is that Texas leaves a great deal of short-term rental regulation to local governments, and the Austin metro is a patchwork of them. Within a 30 minute drive you can cross the City of Austin, two small incorporated cities inside the Westlake area, separate cities out toward the lake, and stretches of unincorporated Travis County, each with its own approach.

Three Layers of Rules

For any given home, three layers can apply at once. First is the city or county ordinance that governs whether and how an STR can operate, including licensing and zoning. Second is any homeowners association, which can restrict or ban short-term rentals regardless of what the city allows. Third are the practical operating rules, things like occupancy limits, parking, and hotel occupancy tax collection. A property has to clear all three layers, not just one.

The Rules Are a Moving Target

Short-term rental regulation in this region has been one of the more contested areas of local policy. Ordinances have been challenged in court, amended, and reinterpreted, and what was true two years ago may not be true today. We treat any STR plan as something to verify against current code at the time of purchase, never as a settled assumption carried over from an older deal.

City of Austin STR Licensing

Inside the City of Austin, operating a short-term rental requires a license. The city has long distinguished between different categories of STR, broadly along the lines of whether the property is the owner's primary residence or a non-owner-occupied investment property, and the licensing path and conditions differ accordingly. Zoning matters too: where a property sits on the city's map can determine whether a given type of STR is permitted there at all.

Beyond the license itself, Austin operators are generally responsible for collecting and remitting hotel occupancy tax and for following operating standards that can cover occupancy, notification, and conduct. The application process, fees, and renewal requirements are set by the city and have changed over time. Because the specifics shift, we point clients to the current City of Austin development services and short-term rental licensing resources, and to a local attorney where the stakes warrant it, rather than relying on a number or rule from a prior year.

Verify before you assume

If an Austin listing is marketed as an existing or potential short-term rental, do not take that at face value. Confirm the property's current license status, its license type, its zoning, and whether any license is transferable to a new owner. A home presented as an STR is not automatically one you can legally operate after closing.

Rules in the Surrounding Cities and HOAs

Step outside the City of Austin limits and the rules change again. The Westlake area alone contains more than one jurisdiction, and the lake communities each set their own course.

West Lake Hills and Rollingwood

West Lake Hills and Rollingwood are separate incorporated cities, each with its own city council and ordinances. These are small, almost entirely residential communities, and their approach to short-term rentals is set locally rather than by the City of Austin. A home with a 78746 address is not necessarily under Austin's rules at all, so the first step is always to identify which city actually has jurisdiction.

Lakeway and Bee Cave

Out toward Lake Travis, Lakeway and Bee Cave are likewise their own cities with their own regulations, and lake-area demand makes STRs a live topic in both. Rules here can include registration, occupancy, and zoning conditions specific to each city, and they are not interchangeable with one another or with Austin's framework.

Unincorporated Travis County

Some properties, especially on larger Hill Country lots, sit in unincorporated Travis County rather than inside any city. County governance is different from city regulation, which changes the analysis again. It is one more reason that the very first question on any STR property is simply: which government has jurisdiction here?

HOAs Sit on Top of Everything

Even where a city or county permits short-term rentals, a homeowners association can restrict or prohibit them. Many of the gated and master-planned communities common across the area carry deed restrictions with minimum lease terms or outright bans on rentals under 30 days. HOA rules can also be amended by a vote of the membership, so they are not static. The governing documents, not the listing description, are the source of truth.

Who sets the rules where

  • City of Austin: Licensing required, owner-occupied vs non-owner-occupied distinction, zoning-dependent, hotel occupancy tax.
  • West Lake Hills / Rollingwood: Separate cities, local ordinances, largely residential character.
  • Lakeway / Bee Cave: Separate cities, their own registration and zoning rules, lake-area demand.
  • Unincorporated Travis County: County governance, different from city regulation.
  • HOAs: Can restrict or ban STRs regardless of city or county rules; can change by member vote.

What Investors Should Verify Before Buying

When a buyer tells us the plan is to short-term rent, we slow down and work through a checklist before anyone falls in love with a house. Getting this right before closing is far cheaper than discovering a problem after.

Jurisdiction and Zoning

Confirm exactly which city or county the property is in and how it is zoned, then confirm that the intended STR use is permitted there under current code. Address and ZIP code alone do not tell you the jurisdiction in this area.

License Status and Transferability

If a property has an existing short-term rental license, find out the type, whether it is in good standing, and critically, whether it transfers to a new owner or must be re-applied for. Licenses are not always portable.

HOA Documents

Read the full set of HOA governing documents and confirm the current short-term rental rules in writing. Pay attention to minimum lease terms, any registration or approval steps, and the process by which the rules could change.

The Numbers Behind the Plan

Underwrite the investment with realistic occupancy and expenses, including the area's meaningful property taxes. Texas has no state income tax, which helps the after-tax picture, but a strong pro forma still depends on honest comparables. This is where Ross's appraisal background earns its keep: pricing the acquisition and projecting income off the right data rather than optimistic averages.

Frequently Asked Questions About Short-Term Rentals in Austin

Can you short-term rent a home in Austin?

In many cases yes, but it depends on the property's location, zoning, and licensing status. The City of Austin requires short-term rental properties to be licensed, and the type of license and where the property sits in the zoning map affect whether an STR is allowed and under what conditions. Owner-occupied and non-owner-occupied properties have historically been treated differently, and the rules have been actively litigated and revised over the years. Because the framework changes, the only reliable way to know if a specific home can be short-term rented is to verify the current City of Austin code and the property's licensing eligibility before you buy or list.

Are short-term rentals allowed in Westlake or Lakeway?

It depends on which jurisdiction the home is actually in, and the answer is not the same across the area people call Westlake or out toward Lakeway. West Lake Hills and Rollingwood are separate incorporated cities with their own ordinances, Lakeway and Bee Cave each set their own rules, and some addresses sit in unincorporated Travis County, which is governed differently again. On top of city rules, many neighborhoods in these areas have HOAs that restrict or prohibit short-term rentals outright. Two homes a mile apart can land under completely different rules, so each property has to be checked individually against its city and HOA.

Do HOAs restrict Airbnb in Austin?

Often, yes. Even where a city allows short-term rentals, a homeowners association can prohibit or limit them through its deed restrictions and bylaws, including minimum lease terms, registration requirements, or outright bans on rentals under 30 days. Many of the gated and master-planned communities common in the Austin Hill Country have exactly these kinds of restrictions. HOA rules can also change by a vote of the membership, so a community that allows STRs today may not next year. Before buying with rental income in mind, read the full set of HOA governing documents and confirm the current rules in writing, not just the city ordinance.

Is a short-term rental a good investment in Austin?

Austin's tourism, events, and lake recreation can make short-term rentals attractive, and Texas has no state income tax, which helps the after-tax math. That said, returns depend heavily on getting the regulatory side right, because a home you cannot legally license is not the investment it appears to be. Property taxes in the area are meaningful, occupancy varies by location and season, and city and HOA rules can tighten. A short-term rental can be a good investment when the property is genuinely STR-eligible, priced correctly, and underwritten with realistic occupancy and expenses. We help clients pressure-test that math with real comparables before they commit.
