Home Styles, Lot Sizes, and Architecture

Laurelbrooke was master-planned by Southern Land Company with strict architectural review standards baked into the deed restrictions. The result is a streetscape that feels cohesive without being cookie-cutter. French Chateau, Tudor Revival, and Traditional Southern designs are the most common styles, typically featuring brick or stone exteriors, symmetrical facades, generous setbacks, and stone entry features at each homesite.

Interior programs reflect the era of construction — late 1990s through mid-2000s — meaning homes arrive with the bones of that generation: soaring ceiling heights, formal dining rooms, dedicated home offices, wine cellars, media rooms, and spa-style primary suites. Buyers renovating for today’s preferences will find kitchens and baths are the most common updates, while the structural quality and lot positioning of these homes is genuinely hard to replicate.

HOA and Gated Community Costs

HOA fees in Laurelbrooke vary by section within the community. Annual dues run from approximately $1,042 to $4,800 per year, which translates to roughly $400 to $480 per month in the higher-cost sections. These figures are above the Williamson County average for HOA communities, and intentionally so.

Because Laurelbrooke is a private community, the internal road maintenance falls to the HOA rather than the city or county. This autonomy preserves road quality and neighborhood condition but is a meaningful cost driver. What those dues cover: 24-hour staffed gatehouse security, grounds maintenance throughout 200+ acres of common space, the 4,000 sq ft clubhouse, aquatic center, two lighted tennis courts, pickleball court, fitness center, basketball court, walking trails, two fishing ponds, playground, and an athletic field.

What Luxury Buyers Need to Know

Buyers new to Laurelbrooke should understand a few things that do not always appear in the listing data. First, the gate is staffed around the clock with background-checked personnel who verify every visitor against an approved guest list. This is not a keypad-entry community — it is a genuinely managed security operation. Second, the architectural review committee must approve any significant exterior modifications, so renovation plans should be reviewed early. Third, because annual sales volume is low, buyers who wait for the perfect home to appear on the MLS may miss it. We recommend getting pre-positioned with a local agent who has relationships inside the community.

Amenities and Lifestyle

Community Amenities

Laurelbrooke is built around the idea that residents should rarely need to leave for recreation. The HOA manages a substantial amenity package anchored by a 4,000 sq ft clubhouse that serves as a gathering hub for community events and private functions. The aquatic center is a full community pool, popular with families from late spring through early fall. Two lighted tennis courts and a pickleball court reflect the community’s active lifestyle, while the fitness center and basketball court round out the on-site athletic offerings.

Two fishing ponds are stocked and accessible via the community’s walking trail system, which winds through the 200+ acres of preserved woodland. For families with younger children, there is a dedicated playground and an open athletic field. The trail network connects all sections of the neighborhood, making Laurelbrooke genuinely walkable within its own boundaries even as the surrounding area is more car-dependent.

Privacy and Security

The security operation at Laurelbrooke is one of the most frequently cited reasons buyers choose this community over alternatives. There are two staffed gate entrances, each attended 24 hours a day by trained security personnel. Every visitor must be on an approved guest list to gain entry. Residents use a separate access protocol for themselves and regular service providers. This level of control is rare in Middle Tennessee and represents a meaningful lifestyle benefit for executives, public figures, and families who place a premium on privacy.

The community’s internal road network is private, meaning through-traffic does not exist. Combined with the generous lot sizes and woodland buffer, the practical result is that Laurelbrooke feels genuinely removed from the density of the broader Franklin market — even though downtown Franklin is just a few minutes away by car.

Proximity to Franklin Dining and Shopping

Laurelbrooke sits in a uniquely convenient position for a community this private. Historic Downtown Franklin is approximately 5 minutes away, giving residents immediate access to the Main Street dining and retail corridor. Standout restaurant options in Franklin include Red Pony, one of the region’s most celebrated fine-dining destinations, along with popular spots like Biscuit Love, Cork and Cow, and Frothy Monkey. The Factory at Franklin, which added an etch location in late 2024, provides a curated mix of shops, restaurants, and event venues in a converted industrial space.

For everyday shopping and premium grocery, the Cool Springs corridor along Interstate 65 is roughly 10 minutes from Laurelbrooke. Whole Foods Market at McEwen, CoolSprings Galleria, and a dense concentration of national and regional retail serve most household needs without requiring a trip to Nashville. For residents who do commute to Nashville, the 25-minute drive via Hillsboro Road or I-65 is manageable by Middle Tennessee standards.

Frequently Asked Questions About Laurelbrooke

This level of staffed, managed security distinguishes Laurelbrooke from keypad-entry or automated-gate communities and is one of the primary reasons buyers prioritizing privacy seek out this neighborhood specifically.
